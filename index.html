The Rapid Increase in Crime During the Industrial Revolution

Nargis Mozafari 9E

Why did crime start and occur during the Industrial Revolution?

The Industrial Revolution, indisputably enhanced large-scale factory manufacturing and operating methods, as well as introducing new machinery, new power sources and more productive and effective ways of organizing work. As a result, there were more goods manufactured and sold.

The Spinning Jenny

The spinning jenny was a machine that used a large wheel to spin many spindles of thread at once

The Flying Shuttle

The flying shuttle introduced a more efficient way of weaving as it could produce cloth more quickly

The Water Frame

The water frame was a spinning frame that could produce yarns of any type

Crompton's Mule

The crompton's mule allowed for a single power source to spin multiple machines & produce high-quality yarn

The Seed Drill

The seed drill was an important invention that enabled us to plant seeds more efficiently

The Steam Engine

The steam engine was a simple device that used boiling water to create mechanical motion to be utilized in useful work

The Cotton Gin

This was a significant invention that helped separate cotton seeds from cotton fibres

However, industrialisation also had a harmful effect on society. Rapid urbanization, terrible living conditions, the spread of deadly diseases and an increase in crime rates were all part of it. Families were forced by factories to move into urban areas in order to work and earn an income. Without a well-organized police force and factories, warehouses and shops full of goods, towns and cities became the hotspot for crime and violence.

Fears and concerns that machinery might deprive people of their jobs increased. Many people turned to crime as a way to improve their poor living conditions and low-income. Furthermore, because of the low life expectancy in industrial cities, orphans were widespread, thus they often turned to crime to make ends meet. During in the Industrial Revolution, majority of people often lived cramped conditions, with open sewers and garbage scattered streets.

What problems did these crimes create for the society and its people?

Analyse

As crime rates increased, poverty and poor living conditions increased. Workers were frequently sacked; police were often disregarded, and hundreds of banks became targets. There were many crimes that people committed and at least 200 were punishable by death. Yet, there were three major crimes; rape, murder and theft/pick pocketing. For women, the most common offence was prostitution. In order to gain some money and feed their family, they had no other choice.

Theft/Pickpocketing

Increased standardization of goods triggered abilities for theft and embezzlement. Pickpocketing was one of the most common types of theft in industrial towns. This was often performed by young boys and worked well in overcrowded streets and places. These pick pockets needed to be skilful, so they do not find themselves in jail or worse. They typically stole purses, watches, pocket handkerchiefs and pins. Henry Mayhew reported in his book London Labour and the London Poor , children, even as young as five years old, were taught by older thieves. “A coat… hanging on the wall with a bell attached to it…” was one method of training future pickpockets. The child then try to remove the handkerchief from the coat’s pocket while the bell is still ringing. They are not regarded well taught until they can do this proficiently.

Murder

Prior to the Industrial Revolution, murder was not a common crime. However, during the Industrial Revolution, people’s attraction in such crime grew after a series of successful garrotting. This sparked public outrage and interest in murder trials. A garrotter was someone who half-strangled their victim so that it would be easier to rob. In the 1880s, a serial killer known as Jack the Ripper piqued the public’s fear in murder and terrified the police. His victims were mainly among the lowest class of society, prostitutes from one of the worst slums in London, a place called Whitechapel. Despite the fact that he is only accused of killing five people, his popularity and significance stem from the society in which he committed the crimes, the brutality of his crimes, and his enduring reputation, to which his identity still remains unknown.

Rape/Prostitution

Women were increasingly exploited in brothels and on the streets in London, despite the fact that prostitution had existed since ancient times. Prostitution of young girls became more popular during the Industrial Revolution, according to a Select Committee report from 1882. Lower class women worked alongside men in coal mines due to the fact they were in need of jobs and money. Due to the intense heat, women wore very little, and the men wore nothing at all in the mines and on many occasions women were raped. As a result, many of the women had illegitimate children. Because these women were poor, they were unable to take maternity leave; instead, they would work all day, give birth the next night, and then return to the mines within a week.

What were the consequences of the crimes for the people that had committed it?

Evaluate

The Victorians believed the people should be punished for their crimes. Some of the crimes were by:

Hanging: was the most severe punishment for serious offences, it was sometimes even done in public
Transportation & penal servitude: where convicted criminals were sent to colonies to serve their sentence (sometimes all the way to Australia)
Imprisonment: prisons served as lockups, it was also places where the accused were kept before their trial
Hard labor: criminals could be sentenced for days, weeks or even years. Prisoners were also sent to hard labour within the prisons themselves

In addition to this, children were often punished in factories. They were usually beaten with a strap to make them work quicker. In some workplaces, if they became too exhausted to work, they were submerged headfirst into the water tanks. Jonathan Downe reported in 1832: "When I was seven years old I went to work at Mr. Marshalls factory at Shrewsbury. If a child was drowsy, the overlooker touches the child on the shoulder and says, "Come here". In a corner of the room there is an iron cistern filled with water. He takes the boy by the legs and dips him in the cistern and sends him back to work."

Case Studies

Henry Catlin

In 1842, Henry was 14 years old when he was sentenced to be transported to Van Diemen’s Land (Tasmania) for 14 years. He had stolen 3/6d (17 ½ pence). His father John had also stolen 2/6d (12 ½ pence) in a separate offense and had been transported to Prison Hulks for 7 years. Henry stayed in Australia after he had served his sentence and eventually died in Victoria 1918.

John Walker

John Walker was charged with theft of onions valued at 4 – 5 shillings. He appeared for trial on 8th April 1874 and was sentenced to seven years’ penal servitude and seven years’ police supervision.

Sarah Dazley

This case outlines the case of a young widow, Sarah Dazley, who at the age of 28 was tried and found guilty of the murder of her 2nd husband, William Dazley. She was the last woman to be publicly hanged at Bedford Gaol.

Lucy Lowe

Between May and July 1876 Lucy was committed and tried for the murder of her 21-day old baby girl. She was found guilty and asked the jury for mercy to be shown, although the death penalty was standard for murder. Eventually the sentence was lowered to life imprisonment.

During the Industrial Revolution, Britain went through some extraordinary economic and societal changes, which then eventually had a significant impact on crime. There were warehouses stuffed with merchandise to sell. There were banks, where huge amounts of money were kept. There were products strewn about that were being moved from one location to another. Burglars found fresh and appealing targets in the homes of those who had made a fortune from industry earnings. All of these provided fresh opportunities for crime and theft.
